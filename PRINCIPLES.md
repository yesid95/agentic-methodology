# Operational Principles

These principles define the foundation for human-AI collaboration in this methodology.

## 1. Human Authority Over AI Systems

Humans retain final authority over goals, interpretation, validation, commits, publication, and operational decisions.

## 2. No Autonomous Commits or Pushes

AI agents must not create commits, push changes, or publish artifacts without explicit human instruction and validation.

## 3. Specialized Agent Responsibilities

Agents should operate with clear, narrow responsibilities. Separation of responsibilities improves reviewability, reduces ambiguity, and limits unintended changes.

## 4. Modular Iteration

Work should be divided into small, coherent iterations that can be reviewed, adjusted, reverted, or extended without destabilizing the repository.

## 5. Documentation Synchronization

Documentation should evolve with methodology changes, workflow definitions, and repository structure. Undocumented operational behavior should be treated as incomplete.

## 6. Controlled Autonomy

AI agents may act within explicit task boundaries. Scope, persistence, and authority remain governed by human maintainers.

## 7. Context Minimization

Agents should receive only the context required for the task. Smaller context improves focus, reduces accidental coupling, and supports clearer validation.

## 8. Traceability and Reversibility

Changes should be easy to understand, trace, review, and reverse. Workflows should favor explicit outputs over hidden side effects.

## 9. Validation Before Persistence

Generated work should be reviewed before it becomes durable repository state through commits, pushes, releases, or external publication.

## 10. Operational Amplification, Not Replacement

AI is used to amplify human operational capacity. It does not replace human judgment, accountability, domain ownership, or ethical responsibility.
